Precolonial Trade & Migration in the Bight of Biafra, 1300-1885

Prior to the European imposition of artificial borders, communication, trade, and the history of communities between Eastern Nigeria and Southern Cameroon were intermixed via waterways, such as the Cross River, footpaths, and oral histories which linked coastal communities with the interior. Coastal communities in locations such as Big Qua Town, Bonny, Rio del Rey, Calabar, Oron, Fernando Po, and Duala were all linked through commerce, communication or a shared history.

Precolonial Trade & Migration in the Bight of Biafra seeks to develop a digital project that refocuses and narrates the history of precolonial trade, migration and life in the Bight of Biafra and along the Cross River. Precolonial Trade addresses the need to bring both Cameroonian and Nigerian narratives into one single digital project. Precolonial Trade begins in the 14th century and ends on the eve of European Partition.

Regional Description

Understanding the Bight of Biafra & Cross River

The Bight of Biafra extends from 370 miles from the Nun outlet of the Niger River, Nigeria to Cape Lope, Gabon. The Bight of Biafra is bounded by southeastern Nigeria, Cameroon, Equatorial Guinea, and northern western Gabon. The Bight of Biafra receives water from portions of the Niger, Ogooue, Cross and Sanaga rivers to name a few. Some of the major ports in the Bight of Biafra include Malabo, Bioko, Port Harcourt and Calabar, Nigeria, Douala, Cameroon, Bata, Equatorial Guinea and Libreville and Port-Gentil, Gabon.

The Cross River, measuring 304 miles, flows from the highlands of western Cameroon and enters Nigeria before entering the Bight of Biafra. Mamfe, Cameroon is located at the navigational point of the Cross River. The Cross River is an important waterway, historically goods such as timber, kernels, palm oil, rubber, and cocoa were sent by canoe and boat to Calabar.
